tips for experience description

  • Do's

  • Do include start and end dates for each position
  • Do use action verbs ("led", "planned", "organized") to showcase your resposibilities
  • Do include numbers or specifics ("Increased sales by 50%") wherever possible
  • Do focus on recent experience: older jobs can just be listed without additional detail

  • Don'ts

  • Don't abbreiviate job titles (use "Manager" instead of "Mgr.")
  • Don't use personal pronouns like "I", "Me", or "My"
  • Don't include stock phrases like "duties included" or "responsibilities consisted of"
  • Don't exagerrate your work roles or fudge your titles/employment dates

tips for your education description

  • Do's

  • Do list your degrees starting with highest or most recent first
  • Do list any honors, awards, scholarships or professional certifications
  • Do mention your expected graduate date if still in school
  • Do keep this section short: no more than 1/3 of a page

  • Don'ts

  • Don't include your GPA unless you're a recent grad with a strong academic record
  • Don't mention high school unless you haven't graduated college
  • Don't list extracurricular activities unless they are directly relevant to the job
  • Don't discuss specific coursework unless it's specific to the job (consider putting this under "skills")

Expert tips for your Skills section

  • Your skills should consist of 6-8 total bullets that best represent you
  • Keep the phrases short and action-oriented ("proven leader", "skilled researcher", etc.)
  • Make sure to use bullets instead of complete sentences
  • The perfect length is between 2-4 phrases per skill (you can elaborate in the interview)

Expert tips for your Summary section

  • Your summary should be between 2-3 impactful sentences
  • Make sure you write your summary in present tense
  • Impress employers by tailoring your summary to the job description
  • Don't be vague about your background or goals—this is your chance to "sell" yourself for the job
